VERDANA

Verdana was originally created in 1996 by Matthew Carter and released the same year. It was commissioned by Steve Ballmer, Microsoft’s Executive VP of Sales and support at the time, for Microsoft as a font that can be readable at any size including small ones. At this point, Carter had been working in graphic design for almost 30 years and started a company with another designer and typographer by the name of Cherie Cone, who helped him in expanding the original Verdana font-family and creating Verdana Pro. Verdana was released along with the Windows operating system, Office, Internet Explorer, and Mac OS.

Verdana, is a combination of the words verdant, meaning green, and the name Ana who is the oldest daughter of Virginia Howlett. Howlett, who is a very accomplished painter and designer, was one of the first designers to be hired by Microsoft and was one of the first people to recognize the need and demand for typefaces similar to Verdana.

Verdana is a member of the humanist, sans-serif typefaces which are inspired by written language. Other examples of the Humanist typefaces are Helvetica, Open Sans, and Gill Sans. Many humanist fonts are described as accessible, which means that they are easily read by anyone due to its very distinct lettering. They are typefaces are typically easier to read for those with dyslexia and other learning disabilities. Other typefaces that are well known working well for those with reading difficulties include Open Dyslexic and Comic Sans.
